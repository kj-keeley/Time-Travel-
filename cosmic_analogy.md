## Catalog of Anomalies

### 1. The Great Attractor

**Observed facts:**
- Gravitational focal point toward which ~100,000 galaxies (including the Milky Way) are converging at ~600 km/s
- Located ~250 million light-years away in the direction of Centaurus/Norma
- Partially hidden behind the Zone of Avoidance (Milky Way's galactic plane blocks direct observation)
- Core of the Laniakea Supercluster (~500 million light-years across)
- Estimated mass: ~10^16 solar masses

**Standard explanation:**
Concentration of mass (Norma Cluster + surrounding structures) creating gravitational attraction. Partially explained but the full gravitational budget doesn't add up — there isn't enough visible matter to account for all the observed motion.

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Major knot (節).** A junction point where multiple branches meet the trunk. The convergent flow of galaxies represents wood grain flowing toward this knot. The fact that it's partially hidden (Zone of Avoidance) is structurally interesting — in a tree, the deepest knots are often buried where they're hardest to see, at the intersection of major structural members.

**Anomaly flag:** The mass deficit (not enough visible matter to explain the attraction) suggests something beyond simple gravitational concentration. In tree terms: the knot's influence extends further than its visible size — possibly because it connects to structure beyond the bark (bulk influence).

### 2. Dark Flow

**Observed facts:**
- Discovered 2008 by Alexander Kashlinsky (NASA) using WMAP data
- ~600-1000 km/s coherent flow of galaxy clusters toward a ~20° patch of sky between Centaurus and Vela
- Extended in 2010 study: flow confirmed across >1000 clusters, some up to 3 billion light-years away
- Direction corresponds with the Great Attractor
- The flow appears to extend beyond the observable universe

**Standard explanation:**
Controversial. Some researchers (Keisler 2009) dispute the statistical significance. If real, it could be caused by gravitational influence from beyond the observable universe — regions that were in causal contact before inflation. No consensus.

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Wood grain direction.** This is the most direct observation of the tree's overall growth direction. All wood grain (gravity) flows in a dominant direction — this is the tree growing. The fact that the flow "extends beyond the observable universe" maps perfectly to wood grain extending beyond any given cross-section of the trunk.

Kashlinsky's own words are striking: "somewhere very far away the world is very different than what we see locally." In tree terms: the trunk extends far beyond the cross-section we can observe, and the grain tells us which direction it extends toward.

**Anomaly flag:** If dark flow is real, it is the single most important observable for the tree structure framework. It directly indicates the tree's growth vector — the direction of time at the largest scale.

### 3. CMB Cold Spot (Eridanus Cold Spot)

**Observed facts:**
- Discovered in WMAP first-year data (2004), confirmed by Planck
- Located in the constellation Eridanus (Southern Celestial Hemisphere)
- Coordinates: RA 03h 15m 05s, Dec −19° 35′ 02″
- Angular radius: ~5° (typical CMB fluctuations are ~1°)
- Temperature: ~70 µK colder than average CMB (~2.7 K); at some points 140 µK colder
- Statistical significance: p-value ≲ 0.01 in standard ΛCDM

**Standard explanation:**
Multiple competing hypotheses. The Eridanus Supervoid (see below) partially explains it via the integrated Sachs-Wolfe effect, but DES data shows the supervoid can only account for 10-20% of the observed temperature depression. Other proposals include cosmic textures, cosmic string loops, or even the imprint of another universe (Laura Mersini-Houghton's controversial hypothesis).

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Ring discontinuity.** A cold spot in the CMB corresponds to an anomaly in the "earliest visible ring" of the tree (the CMB is the oldest observable layer, analogous to the innermost visible ring). A localized temperature depression means that region grew differently — less energetically — at the earliest observable epoch.

This is consistent with two tree structure scenarios:
- **Branch scar:** A branch was present at that point but was removed or rerouted, leaving a cold (less dense) region
- **Graft point:** An intervention occurred at this location, disrupting the natural growth pattern. The 30% deficit between observed and predicted lensing signal (DES data) suggests something beyond simple void effects

**Anomaly flag:** The Cold Spot's resistance to single-cause explanation is itself significant. In tree terms, the most complex ring anomalies occur at graft points, where multiple structural changes happened simultaneously.

### 4. Eridanus Supervoid

**Observed facts:**
- Confirmed by DES Year-3 data (2021)
- Location: aligned with the CMB Cold Spot in the constellation Eridanus
- Size: ~1.8 billion light-years wide, elongated cigar shape
- Distance: center ~2 billion light-years from Earth
- Contains ~30% less matter than the average density at that redshift
- The most prominent large-scale underdensity in DES dark matter mass maps (S/N ≳ 5)
- The lensing signal at the center is ~30% lower than expected from standard ΛCDM simulations

**Standard explanation:**
A real but insufficient explanation for the Cold Spot. The ISW (integrated Sachs-Wolfe) effect from a void this size should produce only ~10-20% of the Cold Spot's temperature depression. The 30% lensing deficit is an additional unexplained anomaly.

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Major cavity / hollow.** The largest confirmed hollow in the observable tree. Its elongated shape ("cigar-shaped") is particularly notable — in trees, elongated cavities along the grain direction indicate where a branch was removed or where decay followed the grain path.

The alignment with the CMB Cold Spot means this cavity extends from the inner rings (CMB era) to the outer rings (recent cosmic history). It's not a surface feature — it goes deep into the tree's structure.

**Anomaly flag:** The 30% lensing deficit is the most interesting number here. Standard models predict a certain gravitational lensing signal from a void of this size. The actual signal is 30% weaker. In tree terms: the cavity is "deeper" than its visible dimensions suggest — it extends further toward the bark than the cross-sectional view shows.

### 5. Boötes Void

**Observed facts:**
- Discovered 1981 by Robert Kirshner et al.
- Location: Boötes constellation
- Diameter: ~330 million light-years (2% of the observable universe)
- Contains only ~60 galaxies (a comparable region should contain ~2,000)
- Surrounded by superclusters: Ursa Major, Shapley, Hercules, Corona Borealis, Boötes
- Sometimes called "The Great Nothing"

**Standard explanation:**
Formed from the merger of smaller voids as the universe expanded. The small number of galaxies inside may have originated in smaller voids before they merged, explaining the thin "walls" of galaxies running through the void. Still anomalously large and empty by ΛCDM standards.

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Clean cavity.** Unlike the Eridanus Supervoid (which is aligned with deeper anomalies), the Boötes Void appears to be a relatively "clean" hollow — no corresponding CMB anomaly has been detected behind it. This suggests natural void formation (growth absence) rather than branch removal.

The surrounding superclusters form a ring of dense structure around the void, resembling the way wood grows denser around a natural cavity to compensate for structural weakness.

**Anomaly flag:** Low. The Boötes Void is anomalously large but structurally consistent with natural tree growth patterns (compensatory densification around hollows).

### 6. Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall

**Observed facts:**
- Discovered 2013 by Horváth, Hakkila, and Bagoly
- Detected via clustering of gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) at redshift z = 1.6-2.1
- Estimated size: ~10 billion light-years (the largest known structure)
- Statistical significance: p = 0.0000055 (binomial probability)
- Existence is debated — some studies (2016, 2020) question whether the GRB clustering is statistically robust given multiple testing corrections

**Standard explanation:**
If real, it violates the cosmological principle (the assumption that the universe is homogeneous at large scales). Standard ΛCDM predicts no structures larger than ~1.2 billion light-years. Its existence is unresolved.

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Trunk surface feature.** At 10 billion light-years, this structure would span a significant fraction of the observable tree. If real, it could represent a major grain pattern — a structural feature of the trunk itself rather than a branch or knot. The GRB clustering indicates concentrated star formation (matter density) along this region, which maps to dense grain in the tree.

**Anomaly flag:** The violation of the cosmological principle is itself a tree structure prediction. Trees are not homogeneous — they have grain, knots, cavities, and branches. The assumption that the universe should be uniform at large scales is the assumption that the tree's cross-section should be featureless. The tree framework predicts the opposite: large-scale structural features should exist.

### 7. Dipole Repeller

**Observed facts:**
- Identified 2017 by Hoffman et al.
- Located in the direction opposite to the Shapley Supercluster
- A region of effective repulsion in the large-scale galaxy flow
- Contributes to the motion of the Local Group alongside the Great Attractor
- The Milky Way is being simultaneously pulled toward the Great Attractor and pushed away from the Dipole Repeller

**Standard explanation:**
An underdense region that creates an effective gravitational "push" (actually the absence of pull). Combined with the Great Attractor's pull, it explains more of the Local Group's peculiar velocity than the Great Attractor alone.

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Grain flow dynamics.** In a tree, wood grain flows around obstacles and toward growth points. The combination of attraction (Great Attractor) and repulsion (Dipole Repeller) creates a directional flow pattern identical to grain flowing around a knot: compressed on one side (attraction), expanded on the other (repulsion).

This pair structure (attractor + repeller) is a natural consequence of the tree model. Grain cannot flow uniformly — it must compress and expand around structural features.

**Anomaly flag:** The paired nature of attractor/repeller is a prediction of the tree model. Look for more such pairs.

### 8. "Axis of Evil" (CMB Alignment Anomaly)

**Observed facts:**
- First noted in WMAP data, confirmed by Planck
- The CMB's largest-scale temperature patterns (quadrupole and octopole) show unexpected alignment with each other and with the plane of the solar system and the equinoxes
- Named "Axis of Evil" by Kate Land and João Magueijo (2005)
- Statistical significance: debated, but the alignment is unlikely (< 0.1%) if the CMB is truly random

**Standard explanation:**
No consensus. Possible explanations include foreground contamination, anisotropic dark energy, non-trivial cosmic topology, or simply statistical fluke. The alignment with the solar system is particularly disturbing — it suggests either a systematic error or that our local environment is cosmically special.

**Tree Structure interpretation:**
**Grain alignment with growth direction.** The tree model predicts that the largest-scale patterns should align with the tree's structural axes. The "Axis of Evil" may be showing the tree's primary growth direction and branching plane. The apparent alignment with the solar system doesn't mean we're special — it means we're located on a particular grain line, and the grain has a direction.

**Anomaly flag:** High. If the alignment is real, it is direct evidence of large-scale structural anisotropy — exactly what the tree model predicts and standard cosmology does not.

## Tree Ring Properties and Cosmic Correspondences

Actual tree rings (studied in dendrochronology) have complex properties that deepen the cosmic analogy beyond simple "one year = one ring."

### Ring Microstructure

Each ring has two layers:
- **Earlywood (春材)** — large cells, thin walls, light color. Rapid growth phase. High water transport capacity.
- **Latewood (秋材)** — small cells, thick walls, dark color. Slow growth phase. Structural reinforcement.

**Cosmic correspondence:** The universe's expansion history also has acceleration and deceleration phases. The current dark-energy-driven acceleration is "earlywood" (rapid expansion). The earlier matter-dominated deceleration is "latewood" (structural consolidation). A single "cosmic ring" may contain both phases.

### Anomalous Ring Types

| Ring Anomaly | Cause in Trees | Cosmic Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Wide ring | Favorable conditions | Period of rapid cosmic expansion |
| Narrow ring | Stress (drought, cold) | Period of suppressed expansion |
| Eccentric ring | Reaction to wind/gravity | Directional expansion anisotropy (Dark Flow) |
| False ring | Growth interruption mid-season | Inflation → reheating transition |
| Missing ring | Extreme stress, no growth recorded | Pre-CMB "dark ages" (no observable record) |
| Fire scar | Physical damage, healed over | Major cosmic event (galaxy cluster collision, GRB) |
| Graft discontinuity | Two different trees joined | Timeline intervention point |

### Trunk vs. Branch: Can Rings Alone Tell the Difference?

**Partially, but not definitively.**

Differences between trunk and branch rings:

1. **Concentricity** — Trunk rings are nearly concentric. Branch rings are eccentric (thicker on top to resist gravity). If cosmic expansion is isotropic, it looks like a trunk. If anisotropic (Dark Flow), it looks like a branch.

2. **Ring width** — Trunk rings are consistently wider (best nutrient supply). Branch rings are narrower and more variable. The stability of cosmic expansion rates is an indicator.

3. **Pith size** — Trunk has a large central pith. Branch has a small one. The "size" of the Big Bang's initial conditions may correspond.

4. **Knot distribution** — Trunk cross-sections show large knots radiating in multiple directions. Branches show fewer, smaller knots. Black hole distribution patterns and scale could serve as indicators.

**The fundamental limitation:** A cell inside one set of rings has no comparison sample. You need to see both trunk and branch rings side by side to tell the difference. We observe only one universe.

**However, one diagnostic exists:** A branch has a **starting point** — a year when it diverged from the trunk. Before that year, the branch has no rings of its own. If we are on a "grafted branch," there should be a discontinuity in the ring record — physical parameters before and after the graft point should show subtle mismatches.

### Anomaly 9: Hubble Tension as Ring Discontinuity

- The expansion rate of the universe measured from the CMB (early universe, "inner rings") gives H₀ ≈ 67.4 km/s/Mpc (Planck 2018)
- The expansion rate measured from nearby supernovae (recent universe, "outer rings") gives H₀ ≈ 73.0 km/s/Mpc (SH0ES team)
- This ~9% discrepancy has persisted across multiple independent measurements for years
- No systematic error has been identified to explain it
- It is one of the most significant unresolved problems in modern cosmology

**Standard explanation:**
No consensus. Possible explanations include new physics (early dark energy, extra neutrino species, modified gravity), systematic measurement errors, or unknown astrophysical effects. None fully resolves the tension.
